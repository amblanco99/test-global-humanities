Graduate Seminar
Borderlands of Classification
Christopher Aaron Baker                                                                                                 baker_ch@auca.kg                                                                                                                        

A graduate survey course, “Borderlands of classification” examines taxonomies in borderland contexts and in those indeterminate spaces in which local and more distinctly imperial and/or global traditions have collided. It is concerned with the problem of naming and indexing things in settings in which the roots of tradition have been “cut and retied and collective symbols appropriated from external influences.” The course encompasses a range of texts that are sensitive to the limits of taxonomic order and to the patchwork traditions that often take shape in the borderlands of empires. It includes works as diverse as Paper Road, Erik Mueggler’s study of floral and human profusion in the borderlands of early twentieth century China and Tibet, and James Clifford’s Predicament of Culture, an examination of French ethnography’s disorienting encounters with the lived diversity of African traditions. Focusing on texts in divergent settings, the goal is less to build broad contextual knowledge than to mine these works for a set of tools capable of analyzing the limits of order, classification breakdowns, the plural cultural forms born from the collision of traditions, and the incongruities involved in reducing lived disorder to indexes and labels. “Borderlands of classification” is part of AUCA’s Global Borderlands project, an endeavor grounded in the notion that literature and history have something to tell us about being “off center among scattered traditions.” The project is concerned with the messiness of being human – with the languages and heritages that are tangled together in human communities and traditions – and with the borders, figurative and literal, that apportion this complexity into more limiting categories. The focus of the project is the confusion of human experience and what does not fit within neatly drawn lines and boundaries. “Borderlands of classification” is meant to help students assemble a toolset for this endeavor, one particularly attuned to the gaps and incongruities in the taxonomies of empires. 

This is a graduate seminar. Attending class regularly and participating actively in class discussions is critical. A final project (roughly 10-12 pages, or 3500 words, excluding notes) is required and due by the end of the last week of classes. Students have the option to do a critical reading of a work or work(s) in which they analyze the methods it brings to bear on classification and borderlands or to write an essay in which they apply tools derived from the texts in class to a document or some other visual or material artifact from their field.    

GRADING:

Grading is equally weighted between the final paper (50%) and class participation (50%), with the latter including attendance and discussion. 

LEARNING OUTCOMES.

“Borderlands of classification” reinforces competencies that are critical to AUCA’s mission as a liberal arts institution, including:

  • the ability to think critically and to analyze problems and issues from multiple perspectives
  • skills in communication and presentation, including the ability to form and present complex arguments and analyses in writing
  • foundational research skills, including the capacity to employ various forms of citation, compile bibliographies, assess and evaluate secondary sources, and to identify materials key to a research project
  • enhanced skills in critical reading and writing through engagement with complex scholarly texts in English.
  • the ability to sift through and evaluate contradictory evidence and to make arguments using citations, concrete examples, and explanations   

“Borderlands of classification” will also introduce students to a series of skills specific to its subject matter:

  •  the ability to analyze the incongruities and silences that inform any effort to transform the confusion experience into lines, indexes, names and labels.
  • the capacity to frame questions that multiply rather than restrict the interpretations that might be applied to borderland contexts.
  • a sensitivity to the power at play in imperial classifications and the ways in which they confine and distort human realities or the manner in which they are bent or broken by traditions more complex than the instruments meant to index them.
  • the ability to apply, and make use of, a range of tools apposite to classification in borderland settings.

In addition, “Borderlands of classification” will instill a series of capacities central to the Global Borderlands project while also reinforcing skills fundamental to the program, the Master of Arts in Central Asian Studies, in which it is offered: 

  • the ability to interrogate/analyze the often-uncertain lines that demarcate peoples, traditions, and cultures
  • the capacity to engage contexts marked by migrations and cultural mixings and in which global and indigenous heritages are folded together or layered one on top of the other.
  • the facility to engage and critically analyze scholarship from diverse disciplinary perspectives.
  • the capacity to critically read and evaluate seminal works and to identify their key arguments, assumptions, and weaknesses.

 

READINGS:

students are expected to study the assigned texts and come to class prepared for discussions. The reading list is extensive but not set in stone. I will adjust and amend it as I get a better sense of what the students, collectively, can handle. Bear in mind that students are not expected to read every word but rather to be able to selectively read what is assigned to understand the methodologies and approaches in the literature.
